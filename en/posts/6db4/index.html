Writing a Personal Site Like an Editorial Journal

A personal site feels calmer when it behaves like an evolving journal rather than a wall of reusable cards.

When a personal website needs to hold research, essays, work-in-progress notes, and small experiments at the same time, it benefits from behaving more like an editorial journal than a feature-packed template blog.

Why not the default blog look

Default blog themes are usually optimized for density. They are good at packing content in, but not always good at helping readers pause. Heavy shadows, rounded cards, and busy sidebars often split attention before the writing has a chance to breathe.

What I want instead is a slower sequence: a large image to establish tone, a restrained title block, and then a stream of writing that feels intentional. The page should introduce the reading mood before it introduces the UI.

Let the image set the atmosphere

The hero image is not there just to decorate the page. It works more like a tonal base layer. Once that mood is in place, the typography can stay calm, left-aligned, and closer to a magazine lead than a poster slogan.

Navigation should feel like an index

The best navigation bars are not the ones with the most features. They are the ones that quietly explain orientation. Transparent over the hero, the nav reads like part of the composition. Once the user scrolls, the frosted state turns it into a reading index that follows the page.

What the article page should preserve

The article page should focus harder, but it should not abandon the same visual language. I still want it to feel like an editorial feature:

  • An image appears first to set the tone for the piece.
  • The title sits below the image like a proper lead section.
  • The reading column stays generous without becoming too wide.
  • The table of contents remains quiet and useful on the right.

A good blog interface does not show everything at once. It helps the reader decide what to look at first, then what to follow next.

Design systems should survive future changes

The hardest part of a personal site is rarely getting version one online. The harder part is letting it keep changing. New posts appear, a research section grows, publication notes get added, and small experiments slowly become permanent pages.

That is why I trust simple reusable rules more than specialized decoration. Borders matter more than visual effects. Spacing matters more than novelty. Hierarchy matters more than ornament. Once those rules are stable, the rest of the site can keep expanding without collapsing into clutter.

What I still want to refine

There are a few areas I still want to improve:

  • cover crops and image rhythm across the article stream
  • a clearer distinction between archive pages and article pages
  • tighter cross-links between research pages and essays
  • stronger bilingual font fallbacks for serif and sans usage

The site is not a fixed artifact. It is closer to a notebook that keeps learning how it wants to be read.
